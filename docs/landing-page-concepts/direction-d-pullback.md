# Direction D — The Pull-Back

> Open intimate: the coach's hand reaching for a phone, one notification glowing. As the user scrolls, the camera pulls back, and back, and back — revealing the coach's whole life around them, then the entire infrastructure operating in the background. The system as quiet guardian.

> **Read [_shared.md](_shared.md) first.** It defines the brand palette, pipeline, GSAP scaffold, and the canonical headline/body copy per station.

---

## Why this direction

- Answers the real objection — *will this replace me?* — visually proves: no, it runs around you.
- Sells the **feeling** of the product more than the mechanism. Pairs beautifully with a "click here to see how it works" CTA that routes to Direction A or B for buyers who want substance.
- Cleanest single-camera move on the planet. Pull-back is Higgsfield's #1 best motion.

---

## Tone & Materials

| Element | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Visual language | Cinematic continuous pull-back — like the opening shot of *Children of Men* but premium-lifestyle, not chaotic |
| Color | Warm cream and deep forest dominate. Burnt orange appears only on the **single notification glow** at the center, and a few orange echo-dots as the camera pulls back to suggest other system activity |
| Environment | Starts in a sunlit kitchen / living space → expands outward to encompass the coach's day → finally reveals an abstract "system around the life" |
| Style reference | Apple Vision Pro promo + IKEA catalog cinematography + Nathan Fielder's *The Curse* domestic tableaux |
| Render mode | Photoreal lifestyle photography, lifted slightly toward the dreamlike |

---

## Master Composition Logic

The same person (back of head, hands, or out-of-focus) is the recurring human element. Camera **never breaks the pull-back** — every transition is a continuous zoom-out. By the final shot, the human is a tiny figure inside a much larger ecosystem of soft glowing nodes.

**Critical:** the same physical environment (kitchen → living room → home → neighborhood-scale abstraction) must remain visually coherent. The room you saw in Station 1 should be discoverable as a glow inside Station 6.

## Six Stations

### Station 1 — Hero. The notification.

**Teaches:** This is the entire physical interface for the coach, in the moment. One tap.
**Copy:** *Approve from anywhere.* / One tap from your kitchen, your car, your couch. The follow-up goes out in your voice.

**Nano Banana Pro prompt:**
> Photorealistic cinematic extreme close-up of a human hand reaching for a smartphone resting screen-up on a cream marble kitchen counter. The phone shows a single soft notification card centered on its screen: a clean Slack-style card with a small burnt-orange "Approve" button glowing softly. The notification text on the card is intentionally minimal and slightly out of focus — only "Draft ready" and the orange button read clearly. The hand is fingertips-only entering frame from the right, in soft natural skin tones. Beside the phone: a partially-visible cream ceramic mug of coffee, a soft fold of a hand-knit forest-green throw, a sliver of an open notebook with handwriting. Late-morning sunlight streams in warm from the upper-left, casting soft shadows across the marble. Shallow depth of field, f/1.8 — only the phone screen and the burnt-orange button are in tack-sharp focus; everything else is creamy bokeh. Subtle film grain. 16:9, 2880x1620.

In the lower-right of the frame, partially out of focus: the same cream marble counter with the smartphone (still showing the soft burnt-orange notification glow, now smaller). The coach is visible from behind, mid-task at the counter — pouring coffee from a matte cream pour-over into a stoneware mug. Their outfit is soft cream knitwear and dark forest pants — premium-casual, lived-in. Subtle hints of activity: a child's drawing taped to the fridge, a paperback book lying spine-up, a yoga mat rolled in the corner. No second person visible. The mood is grounded, warm, real — not aspirational. Shallow depth of field — the coach mid-pour is in focus, the phone glow in the corner is a small soft bokeh point. ## GSAP Notes (direction-specific)

- **Continuous pull-back is the entire trick.** If any clip seam breaks the illusion of a single continuous zoom-out, the direction dies. Audit by playing the master video at 0.25x speed and looking for any moment that feels like a cut.
- **Single-thread spotlight.** The original kitchen-counter notification glow should be detectable in every single station — even when it's just one pixel of warm light. This is the "thread" that ties the whole pull-back together. In the final frame, GSAP should subtly highlight it with a thin DOM crosshair pointer that says "this is where you started." Powerful.
- **Pacing.** Pull-backs read best when they accelerate slightly at the start and decelerate at the end (ease-out). Use `ScrollTrigger`'s `scrub: { ease: 'power2.inOut' }` rather than linear.
- **Final CTA.** After Station 6, the only burnt-orange element on screen should be a CTA button styled like the original notification card — "Book the call" — reinforcing that the user is now in the same position the visualized coach was in.
